Readings → The Obligating Word

Mandy Brown · Into the Gap

Mandy Brown’s Into the Gap is a Woolfian manifesto for refusal — and a quiet elegy for the word that once made refusal binding.

We have some knowledge—the knowledge that war is a horror… Yet who would listen, and how? Where can these words be spoken? Here we find we are in some trouble, for the supreme form of speech in our time is not words but money… When we speak against war we find our words drowned out, lost in the deepfakes and the advertising, the psyops and the slop… No knowledge moves through such mediums, only information, a ravening, unending stream of data in which knowing anything is nigh impossible.

Brown’s distinction between knowledge and information is the pivot on which the essay turns, but she doesn’t press it hard enough. Knowledge, in the older sense — gnosis, savoir, Vico’s verum-factum — is something you are changed by, something that binds you to its object. Information passes through. The loss she is describing is not merely political but ontological: a medium that converts knowledge into information is one that severs the word from its capacity to obligate. The word that cannot obligate cannot call anyone to account, cannot summon the dead as witnesses, cannot ground a refusal in anything deeper than preference. This is why the warmongers don’t need to censor — pollution achieves what prohibition used to require.

In The Third Reich of Dreams, Beradt reports that those who worked against the Nazis had dreams of fierce hope, while those who collaborated and capitulated were wrought by nightmares of terror and humiliation.

She buries this in a footnote, which is either modesty or a failure of nerve — for me it should be the essay’s coda. What Beradt documents is the one medium the informational torrent cannot reach: the dreaming body, where knowledge that has been successfully suppressed by daylight re-emerges as image, as weight, as visitation. This is the humic register Brown’s essay circles without naming. The dead don’t speak in information; they speak in the grammar of dream and dread. That those who collaborated were haunted rather than those who refused is not psychological curiosity — it is the record of a debt. The body knows what it owes and to whom. The gap Brown celebrates at the essay’s close — fertile, bowl-shaped, waiting — is only genuinely open to those whose sleep is not already mortgaged to what they have done in the light.

Cardona, Spain, 9 May 2026
