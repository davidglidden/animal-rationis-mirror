Attention

Whatever brought you here, this page asks the question the work has been asking since the beginning: what kind of attention does this require? The question holds the work and the reader together.

Generosity

Simone Weil names it directly: ‘attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity’. Generosity not in the sense of giving things, but of giving full presence to what is before us, without immediate translation into use. The masters this site draws on share that posture across crafts: Cartier-Bresson called it the decisive moment, when the world and the seeing eye align; John Berger named the seeing that comes before words; Robert Bringhurst treats typography as a practice of attention, not a display of craft. These do not differ in kind. Attention is one thing in many forms.

Witnessing

The pieces on this site are a transcript — a witnessing of how books and experience have shaped me. The library where I dwell is constructed not to be efficient but to be true: a record of what has been lived with, classified by what each book has done to the reader rather than by genre. The work is the inheritance of that dwelling, given form in the hope that it can be passed forward.

Tolkien named this stance the long defeat. Beauty in time is a beauty that does not finally win in the world’s flow, but is preserved by being witnessed and fought for, generation by generation — not despair, not naïve optimism, but the work of witness through time: the ligature held open. The pieces are touched by that awareness; sometimes the awareness is only the colour of the silence around the words.

The work afterwards is to recognise what arrived — to ask, after writing, what quality of attention moved through. The site’s organisation emerges from that recognition: pieces named after the kind of attention they are. Not a system imposed but a recognition gathered.

Reading

Reading is the same practice from the other side. The work meets the reader who comes to it with whatever attention is available, and rewards the attention that stays. Some pieces invite quick recognition; others reward slow return. The framework — the typography, the silence, the apparatus — exists so the reader’s attention can find its own pace, not the page’s.

There is no test, no key, no quiz. If a piece holds, sit with it; if it doesn’t, leave it. The page asks nothing it has not first asked of itself.

This site is one violist’s practice of attention, given form across time. The reader who returns finds the work where it was; the work returns the gesture.
