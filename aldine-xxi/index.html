AldineXXI

Whatever brought you here, the page in front of you is one I have been working on for some time — reading the masters, their voices shaping, watching it hold what I asked of it. AldineXXI is the name I have given the typographic posture that came of it.

Inheritance

AldineXXI gathers from a small set of masters — typographers, designers, philosophers who have thought about the page as a place. None of them speaks for AldineXXI; each has shown me something the page asks of itself.

What came of the reading is something quieter than I expected: not a set of rules but a posture, slowly worked out, that lets the page hold the reader without insisting on itself. Beneath the posture is a deeper commitment — to what we receive from those who came before us, and what we hand on to those who come after. [Ligature]

Ligature

In 1744 Giambattista Vico looked at the wooden graveposts of the giants and wrote that ‘we are sons of this earth, we are born from these oaks.’ The dead held the ground; the living continued it; the unborn would inherit. Beginning in 1817 Giacomo Leopardi, isolated in his father’s library at Recanati, read the volumes as a living speech — viva favella — of voices that, though long dead, came forward to meet him; he wrote the Zibaldone as a dialogue with them, and entered into a kinship with the ancients across time. In 2003 Robert Pogue Harrison gave the figure its modern name: ‘there exists an allegiance between the dead and the unborn of which we the living are merely the ligature.’ To break that allegiance, he writes, is to become a linguistic orphan — to adopt language rather than inherit it.

We are not self-authored. A ligature is what binds two things into a single mark — in type, an fi or an ffi where two letterforms meet and resolve. In Harrison’s figure, the living are the binding the dead and the unborn make through us. Typography is one of the forms this binding takes — language received, given form, handed forward. To not be orphaned in time: this is what the work is for.

What came of it is a named territory between traditions. The Aldine inheritance left the medieval gloss behind for a lean octavo; the medieval inheritance kept the gloss but did not yet know the lean octavo. AldineXXI keeps the Aldine restraint and reintroduces a discrete marginal register — sidenotes, register markers, apparatus that locates without intruding. Neither tradition names this exactly; the synthesis is the work’s own.

Reading

What you are reading is set in three typefaces, each given one kind of work. The body of the page — these words — is set in EB Garamond, which holds long reading without insisting on itself. The apparatus that locates and identifies — the dateline at the foot, the citation marks — is set in IBM Plex Mono. The navigation that orients the reader on the site — the compass at the foot of every page — is set in IBM Plex Sans. The rule is exceptionless: each face has its work; no face crosses its work.

The apparatus does not recede by being dimmed. It reads in body color, distinguished by its form alone — its different face, its different scale, its position alongside rather than within. The eye separates registers by the kind of difference that lets each one still be read. Dimming the apparatus would make it quieter at the cost of legibility; form lets it stay readable and stay quiet at the same time.

Apparatus — everything that locates, identifies, or supports the reading without being part of it: the dateline at the foot of this page, the sources at the close, the captions of images, the citation marks. It speaks alongside the text, never within it.

The measure of the body column is fixed: about sixty-five characters at a time, the line length the eye can travel without losing its place. Narrower is acceptable; wider is not. The same measure holds whether you read on a small screen or a wide one.

Posture

The work is published through doors, not feeds. There are no algorithms here, no engagement metrics, no recommended-next-essay, no comments. Readers find these pages through their URLs, through citations, through the compass at the foot of every page. An Atom feed exists for the reader who chooses to subscribe, but it is not promoted. The reader who arrives is the reader who came looking.

Emphasis in the body is italic, not bold. Italic carries many semantic loads — a foreign word, a word used as itself, a title, a stress, a quoted voice — and the reader resolves which one is in play from context. One mark, many loads. The work trusts the reader.

The work is for return, not for occasion. Manutius’s enchiridion — the handbook the reader returns to — is the lineage. Pieces are written, revised, dwelt-with. The reader who returns finds the work where it was, and finds themselves changed in relation to it.

AldineXXI is one specification in a small corpus. The companions — apparatus, silence-and-rhythm, content-typology, multilingual-typography, vignette, essay-versioning, frontmatter — are governed alongside this one, each canonical for its scope; the Codex is read as one bound thing.

For the dead, the living, and the unborn.
